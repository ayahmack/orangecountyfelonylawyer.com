Orange County Violent Crimes Defense

Assault with a deadly weapon, domestic violence, robbery, criminal threats — and the strike priors that make these cases life-altering.

California Penal Code §667.5(c) — Violent Felonies

California law specifies 23 categories of "violent felonies" in PC §667.5(c) — including murder, robbery, rape, any felony in which great bodily injury is inflicted, and any felony in which a firearm is personally used. A conviction on any violent felony counts as a strike under the Three Strikes law for the rest of a defendant's life.

How Orange County Prosecutes Violent Crimes

Orange County's Major Narcotics and Violent Crimes unit, along with the OCDA's Domestic Violence and Sexual Assault units, handle most violent felonies at Central Justice Center in Santa Ana. The OCDA charges violent crimes with all applicable enhancements by default:

  • PC §12022.7 — Great Bodily Injury enhancement (3-year consecutive prison term)
  • PC §12022.53 — Personal use of a firearm (10/20/life tiered enhancement)
  • PC §186.22 — Gang enhancement (2, 3, or 4 years or life)
  • PC §667.5(b) — Prior prison term enhancements
  • PC §§667(b)–(i), 1170.12 — Three Strikes priors

PC §245(a)(1) — Assault with a Deadly Weapon

ADW is a classic Orange County "wobbler" — charged as either a felony (16 months, 2, or 3 years prison) or a misdemeanor (up to one year county jail). When charged as a felony, PC §245(a)(1) is a serious felony under PC §1192.7(c) and a strike.

Defense strategies include reducing under PC §17(b) at preliminary hearing, challenging whether the object qualifies as a "deadly weapon" (a key evidentiary fight), and raising self-defense and defense of others under CALCRIM 3470.

PC §273.5 — Corporal Injury on a Spouse or Cohabitant

Orange County's domestic violence filings are handled at specialized DV calendars at Central Justice Center and the other three OC justice centers. PC §273.5 requires proof of "corporal injury resulting in a traumatic condition" — even a minor visible injury satisfies the statute.

Punishment ranges from probation to 2, 3, or 4 years state prison, with mandatory 52-week Batterer's Intervention Program (BIP) participation. A conviction triggers a lifetime firearm ban under federal law (Lautenberg Amendment, 18 U.S.C. §922(g)(9)) and significant immigration consequences for non-citizens.

PC §422 — Criminal Threats

Criminal threats is a wobbler that, when charged as a felony, is a serious felony strike under PC §1192.7(c)(38). The statute requires the prosecution prove all five elements of People v. Toledo:

  1. The defendant willfully threatened to commit a crime resulting in death or great bodily injury;
  2. The threat was made with specific intent it be taken as a threat;
  3. The threat — on its face and under the circumstances — was so unequivocal, unconditional, immediate, and specific as to convey a gravity of purpose;
  4. The threat caused the person threatened to be in sustained fear for their safety or their family's safety; and
  5. The fear was reasonable under the circumstances.

Any element missing = no conviction. Text message and voicemail cases are particularly vulnerable on elements 3 and 4.

PC §211 — Robbery

Robbery is always a felony and always a strike. First-degree robbery (PC §212.5) carries 3, 4, or 6 years state prison. Second-degree robbery carries 2, 3, or 5 years. PC §12022.53 firearm enhancements add 10, 20, or 25-to-life consecutive to the base term.

Defense theories focus on whether force or fear was actually used (the element that distinguishes robbery from theft under PC §487), identification issues, and — in firearm allegations — whether the weapon was used "personally" by the defendant or by a co-participant.

Three Strikes and the Romero Motion

Orange County historically pursues Three Strikes cases aggressively. Under PC §§667(b)–(i) and 1170.12, one prior serious or violent felony doubles the sentence on a new felony. A third strike triggers 25 years to life.

The Romero motion (People v. Superior Court (Romero) 13 Cal.4th 497) is often the single most important motion in an OC strike case. It asks the court to strike one or more priors in the interest of justice under PC §1385. OC judges consider the remoteness of the priors, the nature of the current offense, and the defendant's background.

Self-Defense in Orange County

California self-defense law, codified in CALCRIM 505, 506, and 3470, allows the use of reasonable force when a person reasonably believes they are in imminent danger of bodily harm. Mutual combat and initial aggressor doctrines limit the defense. The key evidentiary fight in most OC self-defense cases is what happened in the minutes before the alleged assault — and witness cross-examination is where those cases are won or lost.
